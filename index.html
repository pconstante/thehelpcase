Ablene Cooper v. Kathryn Stockett

PLANTIFF DEFENDANT LAWSUIT VERDICT THOUGHTS BIBLIOGRAPHY

ABELENE COOPER

PLANTIFF

A domestic worker who has been employed by Robert Stockett III (Kathryn Stockett’s brother) for at least twelve years. Cooper has also babysat for Kathryn’s daughter at least once. 

KATHRYN STOCKETT

DEFENDANT

An American novelist who is best known for her novel, The Help which was published in 2009. She has gained much attention and fame for her book, and it was well liked enough to be turned into a movie in 2011.

Court Case

Kathryn Stockett based the book’s protagonist Aibileen Clark on Ablene Cooper without her permission. Aside from their names the two share other significant biographical and personality details according to Mrs. Cooper. They both are deeply religious women who sports a gold tooth and a gold cross. Have lost their sons in some way and in the following months taken up a job as a maid for a family.
‘Kathryn spelt my name wrong, but they pronounce it exactly the same way in the book and the film. I introduced myself to Kathryn when I first met her at her brother’s house that way: ‘‘Aib-e-leen”.
Kathryn has Aibileen teaching the white folks’ baby girl to call her ‘‘Aib-ee”. That’s what I taught Kathryn’s niece and nephew to call me because they couldn’t manage Abilene.  

‘I just cried and cried after I read the first few pages. In the book, Aibileen has taken her job five months after her son is killed in an accident. My son, Willie, had leukaemia and died when he was 18, in July 1998, three months before I went to work for the Stocketts.
‘I felt the emotions in my heart all over again. Kathryn copied parts of my life and used them without even asking me.’
Ablene also claims the portrayal as offensive and “emotionally upsetting”. She filed a case against the author for $75,000.

Court Findings

Ms. Stockett filed a motion for summary judgment, arguing that the suit was filed well outside the one-year statute of limitations for misappropriation claims. Responses went back and forth citing the suit was filed outside of the one-year statute of limitations for misappropriation claims.

Judge Tomie Green of Hinds County Circuit Court sided with Ms. Stockett's motion and dismissed the case out of court. The judge noted that Ms. Cooper had been given a copy of the book in 2009, but did not sue until 2011. (She claimed that she finally read the book in summer of 2010 when the film crew for the movie adaptation came to town.) 

Ms. Cooper was not pleased with the outcome of the case.  “She’s a liar,” she said. “You know she did it and everybody else knows she did it!”

Final Thoughts

The similarities between the two women are too close to each other not to be influenced by Cook. So Stockett is in the wrong for making a character that’s far too similar to her living counterpart without her permission, if it was intended or not. 

On the other side Cooper tried to sue Stockett way after the fact that the book had been published, which seems a little suspicious to me. If she really was upset by the portrayal she would have filed sooner if she did read the book in 2010. It makes her look like now that the book is widely published and successful since it had garnered a movie adaptation, she wants to receive her portion of the royalties.

I don't agree with either side of this case as in one way or another they mishandled the situation. If they had spoken to each other before the book was published and come up with an agreement this would not have happened. Though I can understand where Ms. Cooper is coming from in this situation as if her likeness was used unfairly without permission it would be disheartening to see.
